Q1 Would you describe your program and its history?

CIA is an area within the SFSU Art Department that focuses on digital and emergent technologies. It emphasizes a systematic and structured process of inquiry as an underlying support to the experimental searching at the fringes of the art world. The area has stressed the integration of the rational and the intuitive, integrating strategies of planning and problem-solving more typical of disciplines outside the arts.

Contemporary science and technology are radically transforming the world. The culture desperately needs artists to address these developments. The program encourages students to become knowledgeable about world views, ideas, and tools of these fields and to incorporate them in a non-superficial way into their art making. Students are expected to achieve expertise in technological areas in which most artists only superficially venture. Electronic technology and mediated information distribution seem on the surface value-free, but in fact, are causing major shifts in social interaction and the way we perceive ourselves and reality. Artworks generated through digital technology require some knowledge of strategies learned from semiotics, communications studies, and cultural theory for critically understanding technology's impact on culture.

CIA encourages students to supersede, question, and challenge traditional notions of what constitutes valid art media, contexts, and approaches. Students are challenged to combine traditional media and to incorporate new media and are encouraged to follow their ideas and artistic impulses even if they don't take them into traditional validated art directions. Moreover, the area supports students to pursue their inquiries into a wide spectrum of arenas including performance art, interactive events, public art, earthworks, art and architecture, installation, kinetics and robotics, video, computer imaging, computer generated conceptual work, telecommunications, sound art, art and science, integration with other disciplines, art and language, conceptual art, guerrilla art, and hopefully activities that defy these categories.

>> How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

Conceptual/Information Arts is located within the Art Department, along with Painting, Ceramics, Printmaking, Glass, Sculpture, Photography, Metalworks. The Art Department is one area within The Department of Creative Arts, along with other disciplines including Music, Dance, Theatre, Cinema, Broadcasting, Creative Writing.

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

We offer a Bachelor of Arts degree with an emphasis in Conceptual/Information Arts and a Masters in Fine Arts, with a focus in Conceptual/Information Arts.

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

There are three areas of focus:

  1. Developing technical skills necessary to carry out ideas. This means in terms of both the technology and the skills required to carry out research.
  2. Developing a strong language both within the work, and without, in critiques, discussions, critical writing.
  3. Instigating cross-disciplinary dialogues between students, in classes, between disciplines/departments.

How this is done:

  • Courses bring together technical skills, critique and research into cultural impact, art precedents and historical practices.
  • Classes draw from work in many disciplines such as science, philosophy, literature, semiotics, cinema, and others already mentioned. Students themselves come from many disciplines and continue to move back and forth between our program and other areas such as biology, computer sciences, literature.
  • Each semester, we teach the foundation courses for Conceptual/Information Arts along with several more advanced courses centered around issues or areas within new media. This past semester we offered:
  • Art and Emerging Technologies, a course that aimed to prepare artists and new media professionals to become innovators in the development of emerging technologies. The course focused around questions such as:

    - What will be the art of the future?
    - What is going on in scientific research labs?
    - How can artists penetrate the secrets?

    The course also looks at work in biotechnology, bionics, brain functioning, artificial life, materials science, nanotechnology, rapid-prototyping, ubiquitous computing, information visualization, artificial intelligence, telepresence, gesture recognition, sonification, and other areas of contemporary technology research.
  • Trio of Experimental Technologies, a hands-on studio course exploring sensor and device control, GPS positioning technology
  • Digital Word and Image, a studio course working with non-linear narrative, hypertext based
    web works.

Past semesters' courses have offered experimental sound, robotics, digital video.

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

C/IA is situated within the Art Department. Many students come from and continue to work in other areas within the Art Department, such as sculpture, ceramics, photography.

The Conceptual/Information Arts area is also part of IDA (Interdisciplinary Digital Arts), an interdisciplinary, collaborative certificate program allowing undergraduate students in Art to explore a cross-practice of digital and new technologies in Music, Conceptual/Information Arts, Broadcasting, Cinema and Inter-Arts, an interdisciplinary masters program within Creative Arts.

Cross-disciplinary courses are also offered through NEXA, an interdisciplinary department, offering courses co-taught by educators from various disciplines. While these courses are not located within our area, many of our students take advantage of this cross-disciplinary approach to questions of art and science.

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

In three ways:

  1. We utilize resources within the Bay area within the classroom, incorporating field trips to museums, galleries, research centers such as the San Jose Museum of Technology, INTERVAL, in Palo Alto, XEROX Parc, the Exploratorium, in San Francisco.
  2. Students are informed of relevant exhibitions, lectures, conferences, through emails, posting in the department and are expected to attend events, lectures and exhibitions relevant to their areas of work interest.
  3. Students are expected and encouraged to submit proposals to exhibit their work to both on-line shows and traditional gallery sites. We have calls for work for two University galleries - the Caesar Chavez Student Center gallery and the Art Department, annual student exhibition.

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Conceptual/Information Arts receives no industrial support. We do, however, develop relationships with both non-profit and profit based companies here in the Bay area to provide opportunities for our students to work in a variety of settings as part of the internship component of the program.

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

Students are doing sensor based installation work, experimental digital sound, experimental web-based non-linear narrative, digital video projects, street projections experimenting with open, public billboard spaces, public activism on areas of gentrification, genetically engineered foods. Writing and theory are always a vital and important component to the program.

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

Conceptual/Information Arts is an art program that utilizes new media and emergent technology and fosters a practice built upon cross-disciplinary and experimental approaches to art making. Moreover, we are located within an art department which encourages a rich dialogue between and among the many disciplines here. Lastly, C/IA is within a University system, where students have an opportunity to intensify their educational agenda with courses taken from a wide and diverse curriculum.
