Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

Art Center's graduate MFA program in Communication & New Media Design develops professional design practice in the context of diverse media technologies. We investigate interactive design theory, tools, user experience, and cultural context. While developing core design competencies, our flexible curriculum also responds to evolution in our field and prepares students for careers of continuing innovation. Our graduates distinguish themselves as practitioners, visionaries, entrepreneurs, and design intellectuals. The Art Center MFA program in Communication & New Media Design aims to produce the thought leaders of our profession.

The MFA in Communication & New Media Design is a two-year program. During the first year, students engage with the history and theory of new media in seminars, hone their production skills in studios, learn directly from visiting designers and artists, and devote a large percentage of their time to the Super Studio, a team-oriented group project. During the second year, the seminars and studios are devoted to more specific issues that dovetail with the student's own research interests. The Super Studio serves as both preparation and model for the student's individual master's project, facilitating a connection between group and personal work.

The Graduate Program in Communication & New Media Design is distinguished from the undergraduate offerings in that it is specifically designed to support students in independent research towards a final production that pushes the frontiers of new media design. Art Center has nine undergraduate majors including Advertising, Environmental Design, Film, Fine Art, Graphic Design, Illustration, Photography, Product Design, and Transportation Design. Besides Communication & New Media Design there are graduate programs in Film, Fine Art, Theory & Criticism, and Industrial Design (which combines Environmental, Product and Transportation Design).

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

Mastering the tools of media creation is not an "unavoidable necessity," for us, but rather the place from which students must start if they are to make meaningful work. The program builds on Art Center's renowned design curriculum, and adds technical classes that cover the range of tool sets necessary to create fully formed projects in the new media, with studio courses in programming, interactive design, motion graphics, 3-D modeling, Web development and virtual worlds design. Equally important are the seminars that create an intellectual context for these technical skills. Seminars cover the history and theory of media, critical methods of analysis, interactive narrative and classical story structure, the philosophical implications of virtual systems, and the interweaving of architecture and imaging systems. There are also special seminars and critiques devoted to hosting visiting designers, artists, programmers, critics, and theorists. From these intellectual and technical foundations, students develop a course of research and build their own innovative master's projects and theses.

There is thus a strong discursive element to the program, with both history and theory getting the attention and respect they deserve, and this is where we most often intersect with fine art disciplines and discourses. To reiterate, though, we are focused on the design disciplines.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

In our program, theory is definitely treated as a toolbox rather than a monolith that must be "mastered." We obviously engage with the history of graphic design, but devote equal attention to theoretical approaches that have been tested in other time-based disciplines like film, television, and performance. As design becomes digital, designers have to move fluidly into the realm of performance and narrative. The practice of design moves from a strict regime of visual problem solving into the more fluid fields of experience creation, often unbounded in both time and form. Digital tools and media demand a dynamic aesthetic. Contemporary designers must plan for movement, must anticipate interaction, must understand narrative. While we develop specific classes to deal with these issues, students are encouraged to take seminars outside of Communication & New Media Design, often in the graduate programs in Fine Art and Theory & Criticism.

The program also explores entrepreneurial approaches to the practice of new media design, exposing students to other models of intellectual property systems - the film and software industries prime among these - in which creators have been able to wrest a degree of autonomy, control, and equally important, equity in the projects towards which they contribute.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

We tend to worry about this less than a digital arts program, but some of our students have shown within galleries and museums both while they were in school and after graduation.

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

We have strong ties with industry, which supports our facilities, faculty research initiatives, and students, both before and after they graduate.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

The studio work ranges widely. A few recent graduate projects included: an on-line interactive comic [Brent Wood, www.brambletown.com]; an electronic programing guide for interactive television [Jinhee Park, "MyTV"]; a dynamic typographic adaptation of Eugene Ionesco play, The Lesson [Merlin Lembong, www.digitalplay.net]; scalable information browsers [Chuck Harris, "Monstermesh"]; integrated print/poster/Web learning environments [Elizabeth Pastor, "connecta!"]; interactive, multi-dimensional installations [Michael Sy, "Y O U A R E H E R E"]; and an on-line social issue documentary [Brad Gerstein, www.exopolis.org]. Students enrolled now are working on projects ranging from non-linear digital narratives to the architectures of virtual worlds to contemporary fashion publishing. Theoretical, historical and critical investigations are always integral to the preproduction phase of graduate projects, and are regularly incorporated directly into the final project.

 

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

The program aims to combine aesthetic practice, technological innovation, and critical thinking in such a way that students can craft a professional ethic and a professional practice of design for a mediated world.
