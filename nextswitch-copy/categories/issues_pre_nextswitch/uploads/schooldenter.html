Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

The art program here is quite old, Carnegie Mellon University just celebrated its 100th anniversary and the Art school has been a part of it since the beginning. The School of art has three studio areas. One is Painting/Drawing/Printmaking (PDP) another is Sculpture/Installation/Site Specific (SIS) and third is Electronic/Time Based work (ETB). We also have core curriculum classes in an area called Concept. These classes are not media specific. The New media classes are certainly in ETB which offer classes in Computer Animation, Video, Sound Art, Robotic Art, Interactivity and Installation. But there are many people using new media in Concept classes and in SIS as well. There are also a lot of ways that media art slips into other courses as a result of the hybrid nature of many of the university classes.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

We offer traditional BFA and MFA studio degrees. We also offer hybrid degrees, the BHA (Bachelor of Humanities and Art) combines studio arts with humanities like history or English or anthropology the student chooses their emphasis in the humanities and in studio after selected core classes in each field. The BSA (Bachelor of Science and Art) combines studio with science, popular combinations with studio art in this degree might be computer science or robotics or biology.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

You hit the nail on the head with this question. It is the constant question of how do you teach people to work with technology without just teaching them to be technicians. We struggle with this because we certainly want our students to have technical strengths, but we also realize that it is useless to just teach ever changing software packages. Our goal is to teach the extended history of new media along with the ability to learn software and guide students to develop aesthetic ideas and make choices. We have required freshman and sophomore classes in our core program called Electronic Media Studios. These introduce technology and concepts of media to students immediately and make them comfortable working with computers if they have any fear. It also shows them how to work within the university computing clusters so they are comfortable in Concept and other classes developing projects that use technology. There is also a class that is required by all freshman entering the university that is taken the week before classes begin. It introduces students to primary aspects of computing.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

There are many approaches to new media here. In the School of Art, fine art and activist art/community art are clear approaches, but Film/cinema and television as well as animation, robotics, biology and semiology are all represented in the mix of activity here.

The BHA and BSA degrees I mentioned above are how we formally interact with other programs in the institution, but there is an on going interest in interdisciplinary work and studies. The school of art has a research center called The STUDIO for Creative Inquiry http://www.cmu.edu/studio/ which has fellows from around the world in many interdisciplinary fields who develop and produce projects that have a relationship to art and usually new media. The projects partner with community and academic groups to help foster hybrids between the programs and the rest of the world.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

We have a lot of partners in education. There are many museums here. The Carnegie Museum is right next door; the Warhol and Mattress Factory are in town to name a few of the art institutions. We also work with the Carnegie Science Center and institutions that aren't strictly art related. Frequently students intern at these places, but they are also used regularly as part of the coursework. We also have a visiting lecture series and frequently we will invite artists or curators who are exhibiting at an institution in town to present. We also co-sponsor as many as twenty lectures a year with other institutions.

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Yes, there are job fares on campus and the College of Fine Arts has an office for placement and recruiting for art students. Carnegie Mellon University in general gets tremendous industry support and the School of Art benefits from that as well.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

As I said the studio work is tremendously varied. There are formal classes in video, sound, animation, interactivity, performance and robotic art in the Electronic Time Based Area of the School of Art. However, the School has a core program of concept studios that are required each semester and a senior project requirement. These classes are not media specific and tend to be where a lot of the cross-disciplinary work is initiated. Sometimes there is also cross-fertilization between the concept or project classes and the media specific classes. There is a required theory component as well. The best place to start looking is the school web site http://www-art.cfa.cmu.edu, it is a launching point to all the material about courses as well as projects by students and faculty. An interesting recent collaborative student project is: http://www.appliedautonomy.com/

 

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

This program is a hybrid between a traditional fine art program and an electronic/digital media program. While some of the students who are accepted have a traditional painting and drawing background we encourage people who have an alternative view of art to apply as well, so that there is an interesting mix of ideas and people. If you are doing work with a camcorder, Polaroid, performance, computer animation/imaging or tinkering with electronics we want to see it in your portfolio. We try to make this program about creativity and ideas while teaching skills which cross multiple disciplines. If you don't have a traditional art background, but you are involved and interested in making original work in alternative media that is an excellent source of material for a portfolio.
