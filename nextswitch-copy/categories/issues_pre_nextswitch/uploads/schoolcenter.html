Q1 Would you describe your program and its history?

It grew out of my own longtime research and activity in innovative education in the art and technology domain, and from the world's first university degree programme dedicated wholly and entirely to interactive art (BA Hons Interactive Art) which I created in 1990. THE UK Higher Education Funding Council recognised the work as a centre of excellence and awarded the University of Wales College Newport (UWCN) funding to set up a research centre and PhD programme. As University of Wales Professor of Interactive Art, and the centre's founding director, I established the Centre for Advanced Inquiry in the Interactive Arts at UWCN in 1994. In 1997, the University of Plymouth (U of P) invited me to extend the work of CAiia by creating a research centre there in the School of Computing, so as the U of P Professor of Technoetic Art, I created Science Technology and Art Centre (STAR).

As director of CAiiA-STAR, I combine the two research centres into one integrated research platform.


>> How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

CAiiA is an autonomous unit (independent cost center, I report directly to the Principal of the University) at UWCN STAR is situated within the School of Computing in the Faculty of Technology of U of P, and I report to the head of that School.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

PhD only

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

Research is independently pursued by each candidate. Supervision is largely online in cyberspace. All members of CAiiA Star (approx. 20) meet face to face three times a year for 10 days at a time, either in the Uk or, by invitation, around the world e.g. Valencia Spain, Marseilles France, Rio de Janiero Brazil, Tucson Arizona, Paris France, etc.)

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

Students in my programme are typically mid-career artists whose work is of a generic or formative significance in the emergent field of digital, post-biological art. They are all disposed towards a transdisciplinary approach to their research and development.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

It doesn't. It works in parallel through such organisms as Ars Electronica, ISEA, ICC Tokyo, European Media Festival, Artlink Australia, etc. etc. It is essentially a new field (neither art nor science but more art than science. It is tech-noetic. in the process of being defined though hopefully not categorised or compartmentalised either academically or intellectually).

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Graduates such as e.g. Bill Seaman, Jill Scott, Victoria Vesna, Joseph Nechvatal, each find their own corporate/industrial sponsors.

I am currently in talks with market mediators and investors to establish CAiiA-STAR as an independent Planetary Collegium.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

All doctoral candidates have come to the program specifically to theorise, reflect on and conceptually advance their practice.

 

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

They will need to have made already a demonstrable contribution to the advancement of the field, and give evidence for their role in its future development.
