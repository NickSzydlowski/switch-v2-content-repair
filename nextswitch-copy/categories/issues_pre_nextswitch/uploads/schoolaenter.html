Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

We actually have two programs that may be of interest -- our interdisciplinary New Media Program, and our Digital Art Program.

The Digital Art Program is part of the studio art major, much like painting or printmaking is. Students take foundations courses, followed by introductory courses in the medium, then advanced courses like 3D Modeling and Animation, Interactivity, Digital Video, or Art on the Internet. The focus in the program is on the use of the computer as a means of artistic expression. All Digital Art courses are cross-listed as New Media courses. We have offered a concentration in Digital Art since 1998.

The New Media Program at the University of Maine began in 1991 as a minor in Multimedia, changed its name to New Media in 1998, and began offering a BA through the Interdisciplinary Studies Program in Spring 2000. We expect to offer a stand-alone major in less than two years. Since its inception, the program has retained a strong multidisciplinary approach, with faculty and students participating from art, music, English, computer science, psychology, philosophy, education, mass communication, etc. We are located within the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, and offer courses both as New Media only, and cross-listed with many of the departments listed above. The program does not reside within any one department, but rather through the cooperation of many.

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

We believe that technical-only instruction is a dead-end, as we always expect the hardware and software to change. Our endeavor is to help students become fluid thinkers who are able to adapt to a constantly changing environment. We want them to be able to assess a problem, consider the resources available to them (including technology, collaboration, previous examples, history, etc.) and apply the best solution. We have a very liberal-arts minded approach. Having said that, many of our classes offer a great deal in terms of technical teaching, as it is important that the students are familiar with the resources and tools available to them.

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

The New Media Program is committed to a multidisciplinary approach, as we feel that confining our investigations to one (or even a few) area(s) is limiting. All of our current faculty come from their own disciplines, and came together by way of a mutual interest in the possibilities of computers, and now the Internet.

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

In the Digital Art concentration, our students participate, as do all of our Studio Art majors, in the dialogue about the gallery system, appropriate venues for their work, and professional practices. As our New Media program is not art-specific, the engagement is on a larger scale, including discussion of industry, as well as cultural institutions. In my Art on the Internet class, we have spent a great deal of time on this very question, because it challenges the very nature of the students' preconceptions about art. Part of that conversation deals with the issue of the institutionalization that is happening in the class itself, with the backdrop of Bookchin/Shulgin's Intro. to Net Art 1994-1999 and David Ross' lecture to the CADRE institute as kind of counterpoints in the discussion.

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

We are now beginning to make those industry connections. We realize the financial expense of the program, plus the academically political impact such "branding" might have, both positively and negatively.

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

The Digital Art students create a wide variety of work, from 2D images to database-driven interactive pieces, as well as digital video and animation. All courses include discussion of contemporary theory, and most upper-level classes have reading and writing assignments.

Our New Media program is constructed as follows (perhaps this will help answer the first question as well):

  1. An introductory survey of New Media in the Electronic Age
  2. A hands-on New Media Design Lab, where students take a semester-long project from start to finish, and read everything from design theory to how to brainstorm, as well as reflect on the entire process.
  3. Choose six courses total and at least one in each of the following four areas:
    A. Creative Production
    B. Applied Production
    C. Technical
    D. History and Theory
  4. Two semesters of an internship.
  5. Two semesters of a senior project. In addition, students must take 2D Design, Business and Professional Writing, and Introduction to Programming.

So, yes, theory and writing are a significant portion of the program.

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

We have a very flexible and multidisciplinary program, enriched by the diversity of the participants. We find that the students far exceed our expectations on a regular basis, and are very collegial with one another. We are more interested in the space between the human and the computer than in just the computer itself.
