Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

We have several interrelated programs in Computing and the Arts. We have the Center for Research in Computing and the Arts (CRCA), an Organized Research Unit of the University of California. Within the Visual Arts Dept. is an area in Computing and the Arts. Within the Music Department is an area of Computer Music. Both of these areas offer degrees with emphasis in Computing and the arts and there is also an interdisciplinary major - the Interdisciplinary Computing in the Arts Major (ICAM), a program between the Visual Arts Dept. and the Music Dept.

UCSD has a long history of engaging computing in the arts. CRCA evolved out of the Center for Music Experimentation, an important international center for the development of computer music in the 70's and 80's. In the visual arts, pioneering computer artist Harold Cohen founded CRCA in 1992, and had been teaching computing in the arts courses since the early 80's.

In 1992 Sheldon Brown joined the Visual Arts department to develop the digital media program. He was joined by Lev Manovich in 1995. Together they developed the ICAM program, along with Miller Puckette, Dick Moore and Peter Otto of the Music Dept. Adriene Jenik joined the Visual Arts Faculty in 1997. Additional faculty include George Lewis in the Music Dept.

In 1999, Sheldon Brown became Director of CRCA and Miller Puckette became the Associate Director.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

In the Visual Arts Dept. we offer an MFA, and a BA in Media with Computing in the Arts emphasis. We also offer a BA in the Interdisciplinary Computing in the Arts Major.

In the Music Dept. we offer a PhD, an MA and a BA in Music Technology, along with the BA in ICAM.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

We teach the technology at its most fundamental levels, knowing that the top levels (the actual buttons etc... that one engages) will change by the time the next academic year roles around. Also, it is at these fundamental levels that the conceptual structures of these new media forms most clearly reveal themselves. Students engage in a three pronged approach of theoretical/cultural studies, artistic creation and technological exploration.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

Looking at your list, I would say that you've nailed much of what we engage. However, I would also say that the history of art itself is an extremely important component of mapping out influence and direction for the development of work in this field. This lineage extends throughout the 20th century, but also to pre-modernist and nonwestern art histories. With the structure of CRCA and the ICAM major is inherently interdisciplinary, our program looks across the range of arts, as well as closely to the computer sciences and engineering. One other area not specifically mentioned in your comprehensive list is Cognitive Science.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

Faculty and students have an active engagement with the artworld, although not an exclusive one. The relationship is varied, and often extends the type of activities that these institutions engage in.

Students do a range of work including - installations, performances, digital prints, web sites, cd-roms and robotic sculpture. Students read and write actively as a part of all courses.

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

Our programs draw upon, and aim to bring together, ideas and paradigms from computer science, art and cultural theory. It takes for granted that the computer has become a metamedium and that artists working with computers are expected to combine different media forms in their works. All of this makes the program unique among currently existing computer art or design programs which, on the one hand, usually focus on the use of computers for a particular media (for instance, specializing in computer animation, or computer music, or computer design for print) and, on the other hand, do not enter into a serious dialogue with current research in computer science, only teaching the students "off-the-shelf" software. The goals of the program are to prepare the next generation of artists who will be functioning in a computer-mediated culture; to give students necessary technical, theoretical and historical backgrounds so they can contribute to the development of new aesthetics for computer media; to prepare students to mediate between the worlds of computer science and technology, the arts, and the culture at large by being equally proficient with computing and cultural concepts; and to give students sufficient understanding of the trajectories of development in computing so they can anticipate and work with the emerging trends, rather than being locked in particular software currently available on the market.
