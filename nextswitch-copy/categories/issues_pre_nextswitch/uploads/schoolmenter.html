Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

The Digital Media program is within the Department of Studio Art, which is one of four departments within the School of the Arts. The other departments are Dance, Drama, and Music. In general, the Department of Studio Art takes a wide-ranging, interdisciplinary view of contemporary art practice. The Department emphasizes a demanding, conceptual approach to work in process in addition to traditional notions of product. Students are encouraged to develop an individual, disciplined direction through an experimental approach to media, materials, and techniques. Faculty representing an area (there are two of us in the digital arts area), develop curricula and research agendas. One faculty member leads the area (myself).

Irvine is known for being strong in the Arts, Sciences, and Engineering. As one of the youngest campuses in the 9 campus UC system, it is one of the fastest growing. Over the next decade, there will be more growth at UCI than any other UC campus. Digital Arts are being embraced quite actively in terms of new hires within the School of the Arts (SOTA), and elsewhere on campus. Currently a search for a new hire to be jointly appointed between SOTA and ECE (Electrical and Computer Engineering) was just concluded. More such searches are planned, partly to help initiate new interdisciplinary arts and sciences programs.

Resources supporting the digital arts at UCI are rapidly expanding as well. Construction was just completed on a Music & Media building housing motion capture facilities, electronic music labs, and a high-end research and development computer lab. Construction is under way on the Beall Center for Art and Technology, slated to open Fall 2000 with an opening exhibition on computer games and art. Construction is beginning this summer on a new Studio Art building which will have several new media labs.

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

BA/MFA in Studio Art. A Minor in Digital Arts. A Minor in Interdisciplinary Gaming Studies is under review, and pending passage will be initiated in Winter 2001. An interdisciplinary graduate MFA/MS program is in the works as well, involving collaboration between the School of the Arts, and the School of Engineering.

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

We emphasize both theory and practice. We avoid an application based focus. Instead we stress ideas, and let the technology needed to help realize the ideas follow the concept. We tend toward conceptual and experimental practices, and encourage collaborative work. We stress students take an interdisciplinary approach to their creative practice, and encourage courses in the information and computer sciences, as well as the social sciences and humanities.

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

Which are most influential depends upon the project/course. For example, the proposed Interdisciplinary Gaming Studies Program (IGaSP) will facilitate fundamental research and development in areas germane to games and gaming from within an interdisciplinary framework. Topics for investigation include: networked multimedia delivery; information discovery; visualization; distributed processing; software agent design; human computer interaction; low-bandwidth and high-bandwidth immersive environments, gender and the politics of representation, and content development and evaluation. IGaSP projects will have direct impact on pedagogy, and help educate students to work as critically informed producers and consumers in a transnational entertainment economy operating at the nexus of art, science, and technology. Currently IGaSP is comprised of faculty from departments within Information and Computer Science, the School of the Arts, the School of Social Sciences, and the School of Engineering, and will expand from there. IGaSP can play an important role in helping to provide students with concrete job skills that will allow them to work with a high degree of sophistication in artistic and technical capacities in the rapidly growing multimedia industry. Particularly for those talented graduates and undergraduates who collaborate on core R&D, IGaSP will help give a firm foundation in computer programming and software engineering, communication theory, and creative collaboration in relation to interactive gaming environments. The intention is for IGaSP to help provide bridges to those talented students possessing in-depth familiarity with current technologies, and have enough insight to translate these skills to technologies on the horizon. Such training will produce people capable of thinking in a more comprehensive way about the planning, design, creation, and content of computer games by exposing the most talented to an array of research initiatives linked across a diverse set of interests and disciplines.

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

It engages it only when necessary, and most of the time tries to avoid it.

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Yes. Alias/Wavefront Software has agreed to provide over $1,000,000 of software support to the IGaSP initiative, and recently LithTech, makers of a 3D Game Operating System have granted us over $1,000,000 of software and support as well. Negotiations with a number of other companies are underway as well.

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

Digital imaging, sound design, game design, 3D modeling and animation, networking, multimedia installation, Web
