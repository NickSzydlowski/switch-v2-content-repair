Q1Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

Sara:
The program I'm describing here is the Integrated Media Program at CalArts. IM is for graduate students whose work with technology makes them hard to categorize in any particular discipline. It's an unusual program at the Institute in that it is not situated in any one of the six schools (music, dance, theatre, art, critical studies, film/video) but has students and faculty from each of them. To participate in the program students must be accepted as graduate level students in one of the schools - it is not a full time program, but an add-on to the student's course of study. There are between 30-40 students in the program, the numbers from each school vary - this year there are proportionally greater numbers from the Art School, in years past there have been more from Critical Studies, or from Music. 

The program is made to be flexible and responsive to the interests of the current student population. There are two core courses, a topical seminar and an interdisciplinary critique. There is also a workshop series given by students and recent alums on techniques - how to use the latest whatever. There is a lab/lecture space with workstations, a workbench and tools for electronics work. Each year we figure out what an appropriate model for showing the student work is and put on some kind of show - this year the show is online (at www.integ8.net). 

The history of the program: Integrated Media Program was started in 1994, after much discussion about whether there should be a central media/technology lab/program that served all student technology needs, or whether each of the schools should develop the use of technology in their own metier. It was decided that the more distributed model would serve CalArts better, and now, in course listings you will see courses using digital media in all the disciplines - techniques, programming, use of applications and tools vary widely from school to school. 

The Integrated Media Program provides a middle ground and peer group for those students whose work is not particularly characteristic of any of the disciplines, but is evolving into its own field, given the characteristics of digital media. In the third year of the program it grew to its current size of 35-40 students with six faculty participants, and acquired its own lab/lecture space.

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction? 

Natalie and Kaucyila:
We have practicing artists as faculty, which inherently combines theory and practice. Also, CalArts is known for its conceptual and political approach to art making, particularly in the art school which is one of the most active schools in IM. (Integrated Media). The emphasis of the art school is on "post-studio" practice. Its curriculum is organized around conceptual classes such as history, theory, theme based and genre based and critique classes. 

Practical skills are taught in short intensive workshop classes by technical experts. These workshops occur through the school year as well as during our practicum, a 3 week period in January and February during which art students only take these skill based workshops. 

Consistent with this model is our tendency of teaching 2 part classes broken into theory and practice such classes as "power of the line:

Sara: 
In the last couple of years most of the students in Integrated Media have come into the program with a high level of technical skill. The Integrated Media Seminar, a yearlong class for first year students in the program, takes an approach that uses exercises in circuit building (soldering, using a multi-meter, reading a circuit diagram) and coding (comparative code, so to speak, looking at programming structures in a variety of languages and environments) to learn the cultural history of computation - a theoretical class with practical exercises. 

Some more ephemeral aspects of technical instruction are covered by the students themselves - or recent grads and guests, in IM Studio. 
 
 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution? 

Sara:
This is where the choice not to have a "New Media" department pays off at CalArts - digital media has been dealt with in each of the schools, and in different programs in each of the schools, in profoundly different ways - students are almost inevitably exposed to a wide variety of approaches. 
 
 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions? 

Recently we had a workshop and lecture series (net.net.net) see http://calarts.edu/~ntntnt in which the lectures were presented at MOCA -the museum of contemporary art in downtown L.A. which opened it up to the public and contextualized the students' studies and interactions with the artists within the museum. 

Students' graduating exhibitions and postgraduates' group shows have occurred in L.A. at art venues such has Temporary Contemporary at MOCA, the Bradbury Building and commercial gallery spaces at Bergamont Station. 

We have a steady flow of curators and visiting artists lectures and meet independently with students at CalArts.
 
 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates? 

Sara:
There have been hardware and software donations or discounts from a wide variety of companies who would like to make inroads with our student population, but there has been no long term support or relationship with any company that I am aware of. 

There is enormous industry interest in our students. This is handled through the office of student affairs - for more details write to Wilhelmine Schaeffer in the office of student affairs.
