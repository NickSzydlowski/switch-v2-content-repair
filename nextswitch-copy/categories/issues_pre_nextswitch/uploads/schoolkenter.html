Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

The Communication Design department was started in 1996. At that time I was working in New York City at the American Museum of Natural History, doing freelance work designing games and other new media software, and working as a part time faculty member at the Interactive Telecommunications Program at NYU. I came to QUT here in Brisbane, Australia to hang the detail on the newly established BA program in Communication Design. Communication Design sits within The Academy of Arts next to the Music, Dance, Acting and Visual Arts programs. Our Arts Faculty are about to be reorganized around a $20M++ new facility that will also place us next to Film and TV, Journalism, Creative Writing and possibly a range of other Communication Arts disciplines.

Since 1996 we have firmly grounded that degree in studio training for new media artists and designers. We view students as self-directed creative individuals that require direction by our staff to develop and refine creative, artistic and professional skills.

We have established facilities for drawing and those fundamental artistic and visualization skills, labs and studios for all forms of new media design and for digital video and audio, and 3-D animation. We maintain a huge array of servers for basic web stuff right through to advanced web applications development, including rich media and streaming.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

We offer a 3 year Bachelor of Arts and additional Honours degree which makes up an additional fourth year to the BA degree, and a 3 semester Master of Communication Design.

Bachelor of Arts Students undertake core studies from the Faculty of Arts as well as practical studies in Interactive Design, Animation and Electronic Publishing. Industry secondments and professional studies form part of the course. The course prepares graduates for entry into a variety of existing and emerging creative industries through emphasizing the development of creative and design skills in an information technology context. The student community is a diverse group where collaboration and the development of individual specializations is encouraged. Students graduate with a fully developed portfolio of new media works.

The degree allows students to choose from three separate "strands." They are Interactive Design, Computer Animation, and Games and Entertainment Technologies. Students choose which strand they want to study when they begin the degree.

Bachelor of Arts (Honours) The BA Hons program is a logical extension to the BA program.
It emphasizes

  • Further development of industry based research skills through course work and project development documents.
  • Further emphasis on creative professional practice via a creative project outcome.
  • Further examination of practical contemporary issues through academic research and writing.

We are now refining the BA Hons program to be a "business ideas incubator" resulting in students graduating with a business plan or grant application and proof-of-concept project.

Master of Communication Design is an accelerated course, an advanced version of the current Communication Design offerings. The degree aims to help professionals in established industries gain a significant grounding in design, new media and communications technologies. This new course will meet the rapidly expanding field in the electronic media industry as well as other applications. Most industries are using or beginning to use new electronic media and this course will cater to professionals requiring training to meet the needs of "industry" in general. This broad focus allows opportunity for application and entry into a number of fields and will help the evolution of electronic media applications as the demand for new technology increases. We are currently refining the M Com Design more clearly towards industry development focusing on operational and management issues and using project teams drawn from BA and BA Hons students.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

Many of our staff are part time. They are drawn for the local professional and arts industries and bring a very practical approach to the teaching here. All of our staff understand the need for basic conceptual skills as well as advanced technology skills. We are lucky to be placed within a traditional visual and performing arts context. This allows us to draw specifically from a fantastic range of new and time honored artistic training options. Our students can take a music composition class or a dance history class in addition to the things like fundamental drawing and storytelling skills we teach specifically. We sit within a highly collaborative staff team who are prepared to teach traditional artistic skills as well as explore hugely advanced technological possibilities.

The "blend" happens through this diversity and this collaborative environment.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

Our BA degree was designed with a huge amount of flexibility - students can take a minor in ANY areas of the university during their final year. We have been successful at chipping away at some of the "Silo" mentality and currently we have students studying Science, Business, Law, Education, Engineering and Architecture in addition to the new media studies we provide. There is truly a fantastic mix of possibilities that emerge from a flexible program like ours.

We also have MBA students working on business planning for the new products and services invented by our students.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

I have come from 8 years of working in museums in New York. One of our areas of teaching we call experimental multimedia where students design custom input devices by using a micro-controller and various switches and sensors that plug into it. We also explore the design and building of new products for interfacing with the computer.

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Yes. Our students are REQUIRED to do an internship during the last part of the BA degree and we recruit masters students with industry projects they wish to complete. Our staff are engaged in consulting projects each year which have been museum projects, festival projects, commercial web design, software development, and commercial R&D projects. We have done work for publishers, architects, artists, galleries, media production companies, just to name a few. A recent project was to help write a business plan for a new media production company, define its processes and develop its first two projects. The company is called CyberDreaming Pty Ltd. and is an aboriginal owned design venture focusing on e-commerce sites.
