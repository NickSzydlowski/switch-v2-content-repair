Q1 Would you describe your program and its history?

Cogswell Polytechnical College is in its 113th year of continuous education. Dr. Henry Cogswell was one of San Francisco's first millionaires, making his fortune from land speculation and his dental surgery practice during the California Gold rush. Dr and Mrs. Cogswell eventually established and endowed the college. The Computer and Video Imaging program was created seven years ago to fill the increasing demand for computer graphic artists, primarily 3D animators. We offer a traditional art foundation with specializations in Character Animation, Digital Illustration, Game Design, Modeling and Animation, Digital Motion Picture, Technical Director, and Web Design and Development.

>> How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

They are the core of the Computer and Video program. Our goal is to train digital artists to gain immediate and meaningful employment in their chosen industry. We give students as much art theory and exposure to a fine art context as we feel is appropriate, but our students are extremely focused on going directly into a digital entertainment industry, so they will only respond to a certain point.

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

The College offers a Bachelor of Arts in Computer and Video Imaging, a Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering, a Bachelor of Science in Software Engineering, and a Bachelor of Science in Fire Science.

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

As much as I would like to say we are able to offer a balance between theoretical and technical instruction, we are a Polytechnical college, so we allow a bias towards technical instruction. We try to consistently challenge our students to look at their training and goals as just a means to an end, but these are kids who have been enamored by computers since before they could speak, so it is very difficult to pull them away from a reliance on software as an end in itself. Our curriculum includes traditional sketching, painting, figure drawing, 2D design, color theory, photography, and some sculpture, art and film history, so students are getting a fair amount of traditional academic art training, and they perform quite well in these areas, but they are outstanding in their technical classes such as digital video editing, digital audio concepts, 3D modeling and animation, and advanced web development.

As a faculty, we have to be careful not to project our own desires and fine art biases onto our students. They do not dream of being fine artists, they just want to make a living playing on the computer. After all, these students don't want to sell shoes or wait tables or teach in order to support their art habits as most of my peers have had to do.

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

The curriculum of our web specialization is a bridge between our Engineering and Computer and Video Imaging departments. It is a rare opportunity for art students to work with engineering faculty and students. Our senior project class work includes combined discipline courses such as Project Management, Information Delivery, and Aesthetic Engineering. Business minded students are encouraged to take Entrepreneurship courses such as Introduction to Venturing, The Emerging Company, Managing the New Company, and International Entrepreneurship.

Naturally, we feel these classes offer unique cooperative learning situations that are rarely available in an academic setting, but are a fact of daily life in industry.

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

Only as an educational tool. Students regularly visit museums as a part of the traditional art classes, and we often collaborate with other educational institutions in online projects.

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

We receive some equipment and supplies from industry, and our Industry Advisory Board is very active in helping us revise and update our curriculum on a regular basis, but our primary relationship is a feeder of their employment needs. Recruiters keep our job coordinator very busy. Our students participate in internships while they are in school, and quite often are hired on before graduation. The numbers of our alumni listed in the credits of animated feature and short films and computer games is growing at an incredible rate. We currently have alumni working at industry giants such as ILM, PDI, Pixar, Sega Games, Atari, and Will Vinton Studios, but we also have hundreds of alums working in start-ups and up and coming companies.

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

Our students produce 3D modeling and animation for a variety of targets, including game development, character animation, and film special effects. They also develop web communications and entertainment. There is not an emphasis on theory and writing.

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

Growth in the Computer and Video Program, and a demand for a flexible media education has led to exciting developments in this degree program and for individuals seeking career opportunities in this ever changing field of digital content and media integration.
