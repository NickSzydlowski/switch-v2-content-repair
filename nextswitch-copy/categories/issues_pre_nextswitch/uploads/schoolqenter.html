Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

The Interactive Arts program is unique in its integration of art and design within a technology and networked context. The program focuses on emergent design practices, physical computing, performance based media art, narrative, and networked computing.

The Interactive Arts program area has been developed concurrently with the other program areas, Management and Technology, and Information Technology. TechBC has a focused mandate on technology and knowledge economy learning, and on applied research in each of the three program areas. We've focused academically on innovation through interdisciplinary curricula, use of learning technology, and strong external links to private and public sectors of the community. We admitted our first learners in Sept. 1999.

The Interactive Arts program currently has 5 areas of concentration that can be combined and integrated:

 

Network Media
This concentration focuses on the principles of interactive media directed toward network-based interactive productions delivered on the Internet, Intranet, World Wide Web, or LANs.

Performance Media
This concentration focuses on the principles of interactive media directed toward event-based productions or environments such as architecture, dance, theatre, performance and presentations.

Mechanical Media
This concentration focuses on the principles of interactive media directed toward mechanical electronics like robots, interactive sculpture, interactive devices, digital wearable and personal digital assistants (PDAs).

Linear Media
This concentration focuses on the principles of interactive media directed toward broadcast media productions such as television, video, film and studio.

Local Media
This concentration focuses on the principles of interactive media directed toward disk-based interactive productions delivered on CD-ROMs, DVD, Kiosks or personal computers.

Currently, we have seven full-time faculty focused on the Interactive Arts program for approximately 140 learners involved in the IA curriculum. Our plan is to have approximately, 3000 learners (2000 undergrad, 1000 grad) by 2004 of which a third will be IA related. In 2004 we will have 60 fte equivalent faculty and learning staff developing and delivering the Interactive Arts curriculum and an equal complement in the other program areas.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

Bachelor of Science in Interactive Arts, Bachelor of Science in Management and Technology, and Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, are currently being offered. Masters of Applied Science, an interdisciplinary degree with concentrations in Interactive Arts, Management, and Technology and Information Technology approved and to be offered in Fall 2001. PhD in Interactive Arts, PhD in Management and Technology, and PhD in Information Technology, approved and to be offered in Jan 2001.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

TechBC is a university with an applied research mandate. This focus on the applied is carried through into the academic curriculum. In Interactive Arts we have designed the curriculum to thread the theoretical knowledge with the tacit knowledge of applied learning. This demands approaching theory within activity-based learning and vice-versa. What is evident in fixing an emergent discipline into a curriculum, in an interdisciplinary and technologically evolving context, is that it is premature to assume a defined theoretical knowledge base. This has challenged us to review our assumptions of, in particular, art and design history, in both a traditional sense and a revisionist sense which are both academically driven. The challenges we are trying to meet are learner-centered, to provide an effective socio-cultural theoretical framework for interdisciplinary learners (Interdisciplinarity between the three program areas is explicit in our undergraduate programs, especially our foundation year known as TechOne, the senior year and our MaSC and PhD programs), to address a culturally diverse generation of learners, and to draw on a technology context that draws more heavily from entertainment, science fiction and the short history of computer games. In addition, the socio-economic backdrop is a shift toward a networked economy or society. This has resulted in specific courses that approach theory in a problem based context that try to integrate cultural and technological history, culture and popular arts, as well as addressing a contemporary non-eurocentric view of art and design history.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

TechBC is interdisciplinary at all levels of its curricula, from the undergraduate to the graduate level. The ability to design three program areas concurrently, and in an interdisciplinary way from the ground up is an early advantage of ours. What has become apparent to me, is that it is as crucial, if not more so, to define an interdisciplinary planning and administrative structure within a university that can enable interdisciplinary curriculum development. Interdisciplinarity is a structural issue that cannot be viewed only on the front-end, as co-operation between two structurally distinct departments, for example. The organization itself needs to adopt a level of hybridity that can generate and maintain substantive interdisciplinarity, and can allow for continuing adaptability. The structure requires the ability to hybridize and develop emergent fields of study.

Having said that, as your question makes clear, there are real issues of scope in the area of interdisciplinarity and serious effort and collaboration has to be put in around shared goals and outcomes, otherwise the scope is unmanageable. An institution like TechBC, that formalizes interdisciplinarity, has to formally unpack the term itself, and determine varying levels of practice. We are both fostering and refining between related ideas like, integration, hybridity, trans-disciplinarity, multi-disciplinarity, and cross-disciplinarity.

In real terms, we have developed and delivered cross-program courses and developed TechOne, our interdisciplinary undergraduate first year. The learners all take the same program and decide in their second year what program area to pursue and in the fourth or senior year, they come together again in semester long, interdisciplinary project and team-based courses.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

We work with a number of non-academic institutions and organizations. Much like your earlier question, the level of post-disciplinarity involved in new media has lead to us not privileging one group over another. Much of our external contacts come from interdisciplinary research projects or existing network connections that faculty bring, which is quite varied. Personally, in this past year I've worked with MoMA, the Guggenheim, and Electronic Arts Intermix in New York, as well as with Nokia and Credo Interactive.

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

TechBC is mandated by the provincial government to contribute to the regional economy through not only providing highly qualified graduates but through partnership with industry. We work with a wide number of industry partners from Electronic Arts, IBM, Nortel, Mainframe Entertainment, Credo Interactive, TacTex and a number of other companies. We also have a Program Advisory Committee made up of industry, cultural organizations and union representation. There are an incredible number of opportunities for learners to work with industry while at TechBC through faculty research projects, co-ops, and partner projects.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

At this point TechBC has completed its first cohort of learners at the foundational, TechOne year. Its very early and it will be more clear in the later years the nature of the learners' work. However, theory and writing is an important aspect of the program and through courses like, Process Elements and The History and Theory of Technology and Culture, and Business in a Global Economy, an interdisciplinary framework for theoretical approaches begins in TechOne.

 

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

In the Interactive Arts program we have been free to design a platform for an emerging field on its own terms. Technology is part of the deeper socioeconomic shift that interactive arts is a part of and is not an add on to an existing and traditional art and design program. The chance to rethink an approach to education and research has been capitalized on. This has resulted in team-based learning, effective use of educational technology, outcomes that speak to adaptability, and a conceptual, as well as tacit understanding of the emergent practices for outcomes that will be realized three to five years from now. The other issue to consider is the faculty and our very active research in the emergent and leading edge aspects of new media and its interdisciplinary applications that deeply informs our ongoing curriculum development.
