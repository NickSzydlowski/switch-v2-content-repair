Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?
Q2 What degrees do you offer?

The Department of Media Arts is constantly seeking ways to meet the changing times of new media production and culture by providing our students with a variety of creative and critical perspectives. We're committed to becoming one of the best media arts programs in the country, and we see new media as key to that vision. We are growing, hiring new faculty, and investing in advanced facilities. This is a very exciting time for us.

The Department is located in the College of Fine Arts, and thus we tend to be more concerned with creative processes rather than, for example, communication research. We offer a BA in Media Arts, a BFA in Film and Video Production, and a MA. We are also discussing the possibility of offering a BFA in New Media. Our curriculum emphasizes animation, cultural studies, film and video production, industry studies (with a focus on Hollywood), and new media theory/production.

The College of Fine Arts is working to establish a program in Digital Arts -- with BFA and MFA degrees -- and Media Arts is a committed partner in that endeavor. Our students have numerous options. They can take courses in art, music, dance, theatre arts, and humanities. And, the University administration strongly supports our efforts in both spirit and with generous resources.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

We do not divide theory from practice or practice from cultural criticism. Our curriculum embraces the idea that at the heart of new media is a nexus of cyber theory, interactive multimedia, non-linear storytelling, and cultural activism. For example, one of my undergraduate courses, "Concepts in New Media," teaches students basic theoretical and critical points -- but also has them work in a team to create an interactive story. My graduate course in new media, "Cyberspace: Theory, Culture, Design," investigates cyber theories and cyber cultural methodologies -- and has the students visualizing the various paradigms in Flash. They don't write traditional research papers!

We nonetheless realize that technical proficiency is key to a full understanding and execution of new media, so we teach programs like: Photoshop, Illustrator, Premier, Animation Master, Flash, Director, among others. We're developing a series of lower-division courses that will teach students the "ins and outs" of software and creative expression, thereby devoting upper-division and graduate level courses to ideas, collaboration, processes, and "product." We tend to put concepts before the details and intricacies of software, realizing that technical proficiency is best accomplished in the pursuit of an interesting idea.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

This is a great question - one we've given a lot of thought. First, we see new media as being inherently interdisciplinary, and thus we offer all perspectives (theoretical, critical, historical, and creative). Our faculty come from diverse backgrounds, and that diversity has a positive effect on the curriculum. For example, Craig Caldwell, who is the chair of new media, has both MFA and Ph.D. degrees, and just returned from a two-year stint at Disney animation. I earned my doctorate from the Department of Film and Television at UCLA (where I still teach on a visiting, commuter basis). I studied critical and cultural theory. I'm also a practicing screenwriter. We recently hired Lucy Petrovich, a new media artist, and she'll be teaching courses on interactive design and multimedia authoring. Another colleague, Eileen Meehan, will be teaching a graduate seminar on the political economy of new media. Thus, our program is part art, part animation, part production and storytelling, and part critical and cultural theory.

We also play well in the College sandbox. Our new media curriculum has strong ties to the Digital Arts program mentioned above. We work closely with faculty in digital photography, visual communication (design), music, and theatre arts. And, we're developing a production lab for advanced graduate students and faculty in Engineering, Art, and Media Arts. So, one of the strengths of our program is that, while we work to maintain a rigorous program, we don't erect or maintain "traditional" borders.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

We have strong ties to faculty in Art, and we're working on creating a virtual gallery of new media art and storytelling. We need to create new paradigms to exhibit these works until the art establishment catches up with this medium. We are involved with digital conferences and online journals to both exhibit work and create dialogues with people in the field.

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

Yes, and more and more every year. The Department has gone out of its way to hire faculty who have worked in the business. I worked for USA Networks and the Sci-Fi Channel, and continue to pitch stories and "doctor" scripts (I recently worked on a DreamWorks project). Craig Caldwell worked for Disney animation. Lucy Petrovich works quite extensively with SIGGRAPH, an international computer graphics conference, where she has made many liaisons between students and the industry. Justin Wyatt, our director of graduate studies, worked in marketing in Hollywood and writes for Detour. Yuri Makino, who teaches film production, is a recent semifinalist for the Sundance screenwriting competition. And Beverly Seckinger, who is the Director of Production, recently finished shooting a documentary on the aftermath of the Matthew Shepard murder in her own town of Laramie, Wyoming. All of us work to create internships for our students, and in turn use our relationship with the industry to further support our program. Indeed, our new Internship coordinator, Mary Beth Haralovich, is working hard to create and maintain a pipeline to numerous companies in Los Angeles, San Francisco, and New York.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

We offer courses in animation, non-linear storytelling, interactive design, visualizing theory, multimedia authoring, screenwriting, and critical writing. In Lucy Petrovich's multimedia class, for example, students are exposed to interactive theory, and create stand alone CD-ROM projects that deal with film analysis, post modernism, and self expressions. Our critical studies courses emphasize writing, as we are committed to teaching students this valuable -- and marketable -- skill! Some of our url's include:
