Q1 Would you describe your program and its history? How your new media art endeavor's positioned within the academic structure at your University?

RPI is "the first degree granting technological university in the English-speaking world. Over the last 175 years, Rensselaer alumni have been instrumental originators of technologies, products and events that have changed our world". (from the RPI website)

EMAC was first offered as a BS degree in 1996. It is a program jointly managed by the Arts Department and the Department of Language, Literature, and Communication. It began first as a "track" for interested communication majors (undergrads) who also wanted to take electronic arts classes. After it was recognized as a degree, the program was flooded with an unexpected 40 students for the first freshman class. The institute administration was doubtful that it would attract any students. Since that time, EMAC has attracted over 50 freshman students each Fall, many additional students commit to the program after they enroll at RPI in other majors - those who don't learn about the program until they are already Freshman, during Orientation. More and more, students are coming to RPI specifically for this degree program, and many say they had no alternative school.

The Arts Department at RPI has offered a graduate degree, MFA in Electronic Arts since 1991. Undergraduate classes in arts and music are offered as a "service" to the large Engineering, Science, and Social Science student population, as part of the Institute's core requirement of 24 credits in the school of Humanities and Social Sciences.

LL&C has offered an undergraduate degree in Communication, as well as Masters and Ph.D. degrees in Technical Writing and Communication for many, many years. RPI was the first school to offer a Graduate Degree in Technical Writing, in the country, established in 1953.

 

Q2 What degrees do you offer?

EMAC (Electronic Media, Arts, and Communication) is a 4 year Bachelor of Science degree offered in the School of Humanities and Social Science, Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, Troy, N.Y.

EMAC students complete the Institute requirements in Math & Science, Humanities and Social Science, and the EMAC core. EMAC students may also dual major in a number of areas.

 

Q3 Computers and networks are profoundly conceptual media in the context of literature and the arts. Yet there is also a seemingly unavoidable necessity for students to learn technology skills in a constantly changing technological environment. This seems to demand both a very theoretical/art-historical approach to teaching as well as a need for high-tech education. Does your program have a specific approach to pedagogy in new media arts, especially as pertains to the balance or blending of theoretical and technical instruction?

The EMAC degree requirements are located online at:
http://www.emac.rpi.edu/degree_req.cfm

Although the requirements show that students take 4 credits (minimum) in history/theory courses, from the selection of courses like: "The Multi-Media Century" - a grounding in the art historical stages of modernism, "Television and Culture" - a course that analyses TV and alternatives to the dominant information source..they also take courses in other disciplines. This is not a 100% art course, so their requirements are divided with Communications Theory.

Truly, the RPI student interest is on learning the latest technology and having hands-on access to studio gear. Our pedagogical approach, however, is that they must also be equipped with the theoretical background to support their technical ability, and the analytical skills to offer them longevity in the workplace, so that they are not easily replaced with new software specialists. The EMAC program also equips students to write, communicate, and present their ideas in a critical environment.

 

Q4 Theorists and practicing artists approach new media and network media from a wide range of interdisciplinary perspectives. Film and cinema theory, communications studies, literature, linguistics and semiology, fine art, activist art, computer science, information science, engineering, philosophy, biology, and other fields are platforms of theory and practice which have influenced new media and/or technology art. Which interdisciplinary approaches are most influential in your program, and how is your program integrated with other departments or programs at your institution?

The EMAC program has a built-in duality, that combines the artistic training and rigor from the Arts Department, and communication skills and design focus from the LL&C Department. In addition, many of the EMAC majors choose to be a dual major. Of the offerings at RPI, the most selected majors are Computer Science and Management, with a smattering of various Engineering disciplines.

Beginning Fall 2000, the EMAC curriculum will become more rigorous. This is a result of the programs growth, and from observing student needs. Since the beginning of the program, many courses have been added, due to the addition of faculty and the needs of the growing student population. The Arts Department faculty -- for example-- has doubled, since EMAC began. To bring the curriculum requirements up to par with the actual program being taken by students (and offered by the Institute), a concentration will be required of each EMAC student, a combination of courses that will be selected with academic advising -- that can cross disciplines. For example, students who focus on Animation will build a concentration from a mix of drawing, film theory, and animation classes. They can also elect to concentrate in Community Service, and take courses in the Science Technology Studies program, Arts and LL&C, as well as courses in Human Computer Interaction, (HCI) combining science, psychology and arts. The Concentration is designed to feed into the Capstone course, which is a two semester senior level sequence that requires a research component, thesis and final project that is exhibited publicly.

 

Q5 How does your program engage with and relate to the traditional institutional artworld of museums and other art institutions?

Our students are regularly presented with public programs on campus, as part of the Electronic Arts Performing Series, where top level artists, musicians, and theoreticians are invited to lecture, present, or otherwise interact with students. This differs from the campus lecture series, in that students also assist with the setup and breakdown of events, and have a personal Q&A with the visitor the morning after. Every semester, an artist in residence is on campus several times, and the residency results in a final presentation (in which students participate in the project).

There are also campus wide lectures and programs that bring historians, theoreticians, and other worldly personalities to classrooms and to public programs presented to students.

Arts and LL&C faculty regularly take students to New York City or to other cultural locations, on field trips, to visit museums as well as production studios and design firms.

The Capstone Exhibition was presented in a professional gallery space in Spring 2000, and the Grad Exhibition is taken on tour to New York City.

 

Q6 Do you receive industry support or maintain relationships with companies who are interested in your graduates?

RPI is primarily a school KNOWN for educating Engineers and Computer Scientists. However, our Career Development Center has made a point to target companies who would seek out the employment (or coops and or internships) of students who have artistic and creative writing skills. There has been a great response to this development and an increase in the number of graduates who qualify for interviews. The Spring 2000 class numbered 32 students.

 

Q7 Can you describe the type of studio work that your digital media students are primarily engaged in, and is theory and writing an important component of your program?

Students master a variety of integrated electronic arts skills. They must be facile with audio, video, computer graphics, web building skills, multimedia, graphic design, and writing. In addition, animation, hypertext, sculpture, 3D, and radio/television production are often popular courses that students seek mastery in.

Each course is accompanied by a fair amount of reading assignments, written coursework, and discussion/critique. RPI is not a theory heavy course of study, it is designed in the spirit and energy of the Engineering and Computer Science Schools: hands-on, can-do.

The LL&C courses feature a strong writing agenda, and students are sent to the Writing Center when they do not exhibit basic skills - or are at the "c" level of ability in writing.

 

Q8 Is there anything that is important for prospective students to understand about your program that may assist them in choosing a place of study?

EMAC is one of the first, if not the first, program of its kind which not only combines computer technology and an arts/graphic design aspect, but communication theory and writing. This broad-based background multiplies exponentially the possibilities for employment in a variety of fields.
