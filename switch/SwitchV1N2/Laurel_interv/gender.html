Gender and Sex; Biology and Culture


JC: Virtual reality is a transitory reality. Judith Butler writes in her book Gender Trouble (New York: Routledge, 1990): "Gender ought not be constructed as a stable identity as a locus of agency from which various acts followp; rather gender is an identity tenuously constituted in time, instituted in exterior space through a stylized repetition of acts." Do you think VR will serve to make more evident the subjective and transitory nature of identity? Will the perceived necessity to define gender become unimportant?

BL: That's already true in some ways. I mean, we've already seen that on the cover of Newsweek magazine. ... It's certainly true that the performative aspects of gender come into focus when you're working in an environment where you're representing the self as an avatar. It involves the unexpected construction of a self that can be more intimate than we're allowed to present in more traditional, actual-world social settings.

I think this is important. I've been doing some work in this area over the past couple of years. I believe that there are actual brain-based and physiological differences between the sexes.

It's not as easy as saying 'I'll just put on my boy hat now.' The dance between biological and cultural differences is not well understood, and I think a certain flavor of feminist tends to de-emphasize the biological aspect and that's not a particularly good thing to do if you're trying to intervene.

JC: Perhaps what needs to be happening is addressing the issues, researching them, not just discounting them conclusively on the basis of political reasons.
