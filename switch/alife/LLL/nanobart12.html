Nanoworld

There's a lot "out there" about nanotechnology. I've only scratched the surface with my exploration of a few points of view.

Drexler's Foresight Institute continues to monitor the progress of nanotechnology with its conferences, the first of which was held in 1989. So far there have been several conferences, and another coming up. These conferences bring together scientific researchers (chemists, engineers, physicists), business executives, environmentalists, and others interested the social and political potentials of nanotechnology. Futurists and scientists are examining and plotting the future of our world.

Where are the artists? There is one corporation, Nanothinc which claims to One of Nanothinc's objectives is to connect science and technology with art and culture:

"Our company strives to globally link the power of imagination and creativity, expressed in rich and dramatic art forms, with the literal underpinnings of this powerful yet nascent technology.
Building bridges, both in cultural and scientific spheres, we believe is of considerable importancand a worthy corporate endeavour."
Their web site offers an idea of their progress and focus. Their nanogallery provides a peek at some artists who are dealing with this issue.

In 1992 Drexler was brought as an expert witness on molecular nanotechnology before the, Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, Subcommittee on Science, Technology and Space. When pressed for a date by then-Senator Gore during his testimony, he stated "...fifteen years would not be surprising for major large-scale applications." That's 2007.

Koruga predicts, using his "temporal icosahedron model" based on the C60 molecule, that in 2020 "technology and engineering based on the C60 molecule will beome a dominant cosmic 'light machine' which will reversibly transform human information directly to machine and vice versa." If this sounds like science fiction, once upon a time so did a lot of other things that we take for granted today.

Nanotechnology offers many positive and negative potentials for our future. As Jerry Mander in Absence of the Sacred points out, Dupont's promise of Better things for better living through chemistry, and Westinghouse's There's a lift to living electronically, have not exactly brought us utopia. In regard to current technologies, Mander states (with the benefit of hindsight), "...we are at an appropropriate moment to question whether this path we have chosen and celebrated has lived up to its promise, and if not, if it ever will."

Nanotechnology is an infant technology, the path has not yet been chosen. Langdon Winner in The Whale and the Reactor notes, "We are seldom inclined to examine, discuss or judge pending innovations."

Perhaps it's time.
